THE CHALLENGE

People know they should budget. Most apps make it feel like homework.

Traditional budgeting tools demand bank connections, personal information, and complex setup. Users abandon them within 30 days. I wanted to prove that AI could eliminate friction—no bank access, no forms, no learning curve. Just scan a receipt or upload a statement, and instantly understand where your money goes.

The real challenge: making budgeting feel effortless while providing enterprise-level insights.

Architecture Decisions

Privacy-First Design

No bank linking required. Only country for currency. Local-first data with optional cloud sync. 23% of users choose privacy mode.

AI Categorization Engine

Gemini 1.5 Pro + custom rules for 50+ categories. Learning algorithm improves with user corrections. 94% auto-categorization accuracy.

Real-Time Family Sync

Supabase real-time subscriptions. Instant updates across devices. Role-based permissions. Offline support with conflict resolution.

REFLECTION

Key Learnings

What I'd do differently and what validated my approach.

Privacy Is a Feature, Not a Constraint

I worried that not requiring bank access would limit functionality. The opposite happened—users chose my app specifically because of it. Privacy became the primary differentiator.

→ Validate: Constraints can become competitive advantages

Family Dynamics Require Specialized UX

Shared budgets aren't just "multiple users." They're about trust and transparency. I added "spending notes" ("This was for mom's birthday") that reduced family friction.

→ Validate: Design for relationships, not just functionality

AI Accuracy Matters Less Than AI Recovery

Users forgive miscategorization if fixing it is effortless. A single-tap "Change Category" with learning ("Got it, Starbucks is usually Coffee") built trust over time.

→ Validate: Design for graceful failure

Visual Progress Drives Behavior Change

The circular budget progress indicator was the most-viewed screen. Making spending visible—not just trackable—drove the 40% reduction in overspending.

→ Validate: Show, don't just tell

"Budgeting tools fail when they ask users to change their behavior. I built something that adapts to how people already manage money—casually, socially, and with minimal effort. The 40% spending reduction wasn't from stricter budgets. It was from making spending visible in real-time, with the people who matter."

— My takeaway from this project
