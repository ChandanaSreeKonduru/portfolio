FIRST, WHAT IS PHARMACOVIGILANCE?

The Science of Drug Safety: Detecting Harm Before It Spreads

Pharmacovigilance (PV) is the science and activities related to detecting, assessing, understanding, and preventing adverse effects or any other drug-related problems. When a patient reports a side effect, a medical reviewer must determine: Is this drug causing harm? How serious is it? Should regulators be alerted?

The challenge: Medical reviewers face complex data management from thousands of sources, timely reporting pressures, and stringent regulatory compliance requirements (E2B R2/R3 standards for electronic case submission).

The AI Trust Problem in Healthcare

Medical reviewers are experts with years of training. They won't accept AI recommendations unless they understand why the AI made its decision. "Just because the AI can do something, doesn't mean it should. Good UX design keeps things clear, helpful, and grounded in real human needs."

My challenge: Design interfaces that make AI recommendations explainable, contestable, and trustworthy—while maintaining the speed and consistency benefits of automation.

THE CHALLENGE

Medical reviewers were drowning in case volume—and drowning out the AI that could help them.

Data Foundry's DF Safety platform served Clinical Research Organizations (CROs) and pharmaceutical companies with a multi-vigilance solution covering Drugs, Vaccines, Cosmetics, and Medical Devices. But the AI capabilities were underutilized: medical reviewers didn't trust automated case creation, questioned AI-generated MedDRA codes, and manually overrode 70% of seriousness predictions.

The underlying AI was accurate. The interface was the problem. Reviewers saw black-box outputs with no explanation, no confidence scores, no way to efficiently validate or correct. My mandate: Redesign the human-AI collaboration layer to make machine learning a trustworthy partner for medical experts.

Medical Reviewer Research: 15 Interviews, 3 CROs

I spent 3 weeks embedded with medical reviewers at Clinical Research Organizations, observing their workflows and probing their AI skepticism:

  • Trust barrier: "I can't defend this decision to a regulator if I don't understand how the AI got here."
  • Control need: "The AI is fast but sloppy. I'd rather be slow and right."
  • Explanation gap: Current interface showed AI conclusion, not AI reasoning (which features triggered the prediction?)
  • Correction friction: Overriding AI required 12+ clicks; easier to start from scratch
  • Regulatory anxiety: E2B submission errors could trigger FDA findings; reviewers feared AI would miss compliance nuances
"I'm not against AI. I'm against AI I can't interrogate. Give me the reasoning, give me the confidence level, give me one-click correction—and I'll use it."
— Senior Medical Reviewer, Top 10 CRO

Explainable AI (XAI) Literature Review

Before designing, I synthesized 47 academic articles on AI explanation interfaces for clinical decision support. Key insights that shaped my approach:

What Information to Provide?

  • Feature importance (which case characteristics drove the prediction?)
  • Confidence scores (how certain is the AI?)
  • Similar cases (what past cases resemble this one?)
  • Model limitations (what does the AI not know?)

How to Design the Interface?

  • Layered explanations: high-level summary → detailed reasoning → raw features
  • Visual hierarchies: Make AI reasoning scannable, not buried
  • Interactive exploration: Let reviewers drill into specific factors
  • Plain language: Avoid technical model jargon

How to Handle Errors?

  • Design for "oh-oh" moments: What happens when AI is wrong?
  • Make correction effortless: One-click override with explanation
  • Provide second opinions: Alternative predictions with confidence
  • Never trap users: Always provide manual path

Core Design Principles for Medical AI

🔍 Make Things Explainable

If AI suggests a case is serious, show exactly which criteria matched (hospitalization? life-threatening? disability?). Use plain language. Display confidence scores. "Design like your goal is to build trust with every click."

🤝 Design for Collaboration, Not Control

AI cannot replace medical reviewers. It assists them. Make it easy to accept, question, or override AI suggestions. Never force AI decisions. "AI can be their assistant. Make it easy for users to accept, question, or override the AI's suggestions."

⚠️ Plan for the "Oh-Oh"

What happens when AI miscodes a serious adverse event? Build in failsafes. Provide second opinions. Don't trap reviewers on a single path. "Build in failsafes. Provide second opinions. Don't trap users on a single path."

✅ Keep It Simple

Even if AI logic is complex (neural networks, ensemble models), the interface shouldn't be. Prioritize clarity over cleverness. Medical reviewers work under time pressure and regulatory anxiety.

Multi-Vigilance Unification Strategy

The Problem: Four Silos, Four UX Patterns

DF Safety handled Drugs, Vaccines, Cosmetics, and Medical Devices—but each had separate workflows, terminology, and interfaces. CROs managing multiple product types needed to train staff on four different systems.

The Solution: Unified Platform with Contextual Adaptation

I designed a single interface framework that adapts based on product type:

  • Shared structure: Case intake → AI triage → Review → Signal detection → Report
  • Contextual fields: Drugs show "indication," vaccines show "lot number," devices show "UDI"
  • Consistent AI patterns: Same explanation interface, adapted to product-specific seriousness criteria
  • Result: 40% reduction in training time for multi-product CROs

Signal Detection: From Data to Decision

Demand Science Approach

Applied "Demand Science" principles to transform complex ML outputs and large datasets into actionable outcomes:

  • Disproportionality analysis: Statistical algorithms identify signals, but reviewers need stories
  • UX translation: "Drug X + Event Y: 3× higher than expected based on 10,000 cases. Emerging trend over 6 months."
  • Multi-format dashboards: Executive view (trends), Medical view (case details), Regulatory view (compliance status)

Risk Management Interface

Designed collaborative signal management:

  • Shared workspace for cross-functional teams (medical, regulatory, statistics)
  • Threaded discussions attached to specific signals
  • Regulatory report generation: One-click E2B R3 signal documentation
  • Audit trail: Every decision tracked for inspections

Regulatory Compliance: E2B R2/R3 Design

The Compliance Challenge

E2B (Electronic Transmission of Individual Case Safety Reports) is the global standard for regulatory submission. E2B R3 introduced more granular data fields and improved structure—but also complexity. My design had to ensure:

  • Data integrity: AI-extracted fields must be traceable to source
  • Null flavor handling: Proper handling of missing data per E2B standards
  • Backwards compatibility: Support both R2 and R3 during transition period
  • Audit trails: Every AI decision and human correction logged

UX Solutions for Compliance

Source-Linked Data Entry

Every AI-extracted field links to source document location. Reviewer can click to verify context.

Validation Visual Indicators

Real-time E2B validation: Green check for compliant, yellow warning for missing optional, red error for missing required.

Bulk XML Migration Interface

Designed seamless data import/export workflows including bulk XML migration with progress tracking and error reporting.

Audit-Ready History

Complete decision log: AI suggestion → Reviewer action → Final value → Timestamp → User ID. Exportable for inspections.

REFLECTION

Key Learnings

What designing AI for medical experts taught me about trust, transparency, and regulation.

Trust Is the Real KPI for Healthcare AI

We measured efficiency gains (3% here, 3% there), but the real metric was trust. When AI acceptance jumped from 30% to 85%, reviewers weren't just faster—they were more confident, more consistent, and more willing to use advanced features. "Measure trust, not just clicks. While engagement metrics are useful, trust is the real key performance indicator (KPI) in healthcare."

→ Validate: In regulated industries, trust metrics predict adoption better than speed metrics

Explainability Requires Layered Design

One layer of explanation doesn't fit all. New reviewers want simple confidence scores. Senior reviewers want feature-level details. Regulators want audit trails. I designed progressive disclosure: summary → criteria → similar cases → raw features. Each user drills to their comfort level.

→ Validate: Design explanations as journeys, not monoliths

Compliance Is a UX Feature, Not a Constraint

E2B R2/R3 requirements initially felt like design limitations. But when I made audit trails visible and source-linking interactive, reviewers found them valuable for their own verification—not just regulatory box-checking. Compliance features became trust features.

→ Validate: Regulatory requirements often align with user needs for transparency

AI-UX Design Is Cross-Functional Diplomacy

Data scientists wanted to show model accuracy. Medical reviewers wanted to see clinical reasoning. Regulatory wanted audit trails. I had to synthesize these into coherent interfaces—translating between technical metrics, clinical concepts, and compliance requirements. The best AI product designers are multilingual.

→ Validate: AI UX requires fluency in data science, domain expertise, and regulation

"Designing AI for medical reviewers taught me that the most sophisticated machine learning is worthless if humans can't understand, trust, and efficiently correct it. The 3% efficiency gains in case processing weren't just about speed—they represented hours reclaimed for clinical thinking. The 85% AI acceptance rate wasn't just adoption—it was trust earned through transparency. In healthcare AI, the interface is the product. My job wasn't to make the AI smarter; it was to make the collaboration between human expertise and machine intelligence seamless, defensible, and regulation-ready."

— My takeaway from this project
