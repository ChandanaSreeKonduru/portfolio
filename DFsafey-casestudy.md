                <div class="design-principles">
                    <h3>Core Design Principles for Medical AI</h3>
                    <div class="principles-grid">
                        <div class="principle-card">
                            <h4>🔍 Make Things Explainable</h4>
                            <p>If AI suggests a case is serious, show exactly which criteria matched (hospitalization? life-threatening? disability?). Use plain language. Display confidence scores. "Design like your goal is to build trust with every click." </p>
                        </div>
                        <div class="principle-card">
                            <h4>🤝 Design for Collaboration, Not Control</h4>
                            <p>AI cannot replace medical reviewers. It assists them. Make it easy to accept, question, or override AI suggestions. Never force AI decisions. "AI can be their assistant. Make it easy for users to accept, question, or override the AI's suggestions." </p>
                        </div>
                        <div class="principle-card">
                            <h4>⚠️ Plan for the "Oh-Oh"</h4>
                            <p>What happens when AI miscodes a serious adverse event? Build in failsafes. Provide second opinions. Don't trap reviewers on a single path. "Build in failsafes. Provide second opinions. Don't trap users on a single path." </p>
                        </div>
                        <div class="principle-card">
                            <h4>✅ Keep It Simple</h4>
                            <p>Even if AI logic is complex (neural networks, ensemble models), the interface shouldn't be. Prioritize clarity over cleverness. Medical reviewers work under time pressure and regulatory anxiety.</p>
                        </div>
                    </div>

                <div class="unification-strategy">

                        <p>DF Safety handled Drugs, Vaccines, Cosmetics, and Medical Devices—but each had separate workflows, terminology, and interfaces. CROs managing multiple product types needed to train staff on four different systems.</p>
                    </div>
                    <div class="strategy-right">
                        <h4>The Solution: Unified Platform with Contextual Adaptation</h4>
                        <p>I designed a single interface framework that adapts based on product type:</p>
                        <ul>
                            <li><strong>Shared structure:</strong> Case intake → AI triage → Review → Signal detection → Report</li>
                            <li><strong>Contextual fields:</strong> Drugs show "indication," vaccines show "lot number," devices show "UDI"</li>
                            <li><strong>Consistent AI patterns:</strong> Same explanation interface, adapted to product-specific seriousness criteria</li>

                        <h4>Trust Is the Real KPI for Healthcare AI</h4>
                        <p>We measured efficiency gains (3% here, 3% there), but the real metric was trust. When AI acceptance jumped from 30% to 85%, reviewers weren't just faster—they were more confident, more consistent, and more willing to use advanced features. "Measure trust, not just clicks. While engagement metrics are useful, trust is the real key performance indicator (KPI) in healthcare." </p>
                        <span class="learning-caption">→ Validate: In regulated industries, trust metrics predict adoption better than speed metrics</span>
                    </div>
                    <div class="learning-card">
                        <h4>Explainability Requires Layered Design</h4>
                        <p>One layer of explanation doesn't fit all. New reviewers want simple confidence scores. Senior reviewers want feature-level details. Regulators want audit trails. I designed progressive disclosure: summary → criteria → similar cases → raw features. Each user drills to their comfort level.</p>
                        <span class="learning-caption">→ Validate: Design explanations as journeys, not monoliths</span>
                    </div>
                    <div class="learning-card">
                        <h4>Compliance Is a UX Feature, Not a Constraint</h4>
                        <p>E2B R2/R3 requirements initially felt like design limitations. But when I made audit trails visible and source-linking interactive, reviewers found them valuable for their own verification—not just regulatory box-checking. Compliance features became trust features.</p>
                        <span class="learning-caption">→ Validate: Regulatory requirements often align with user needs for transparency</span>
                    </div>
                    <div class="learning-card">
                        <h4>AI-UX Design Is Cross-Functional Diplomacy</h4>
                        <p>Data scientists wanted to show model accuracy. Medical reviewers wanted to see clinical reasoning. Regulatory wanted audit trails. I had to synthesize these into coherent interfaces—translating between technical metrics, clinical concepts, and compliance requirements. The best AI product designers are multilingual.</p>
